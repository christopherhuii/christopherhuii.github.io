Blog #3: Borders vs Margins vs Padding

9/6/2014
12:00PM

When I first started learning HTML/CSS, one of the fundamental concepts that went overlooked was margins, borders, and padding. While they may sound like the same function, understanding all of these properties can help reduce the amount of frustration when trying to figure out why your webpage isn’t formatting correctly. Before we dive into these concepts, take a look at this image and try to figure out what is going on.

As you may or may not have noticed, everything is contained within a box. This is because HTML/CSS follows the Box Model. The box model states that each HTML element is contained within a box. Any words or image on HTML is contained within a box. These words I am typing are contained within a box. Simple enough, right? Let’s move on.

Any words I type or images I put on this page are going to be contained within a box, right? Yes! These words and/or images are called the content. That should be pretty simple to understand. The content is the information that you are adding to the page. Surrounding the content is going to be the border. The border is is what is going to be surrounding the content. Using CSS, we can adjust all sorts of different properties with borders. We can make them blue, red, fat, skinny, solid, dashed, etc. We’ve all seen borders and what they look like, so there isn’t much more to explain. Before we get into the next section, take a look at this image and see if you can figure out what the next property is!

The next property we’re going to look at is the padding. The padding is the space that is in-between the CONTENT and the BORDER. I want to emphasize rereading that last sentence a few times before moving on. Then take a look at the image above and visualize what the padding is. We can see that there is an image (which is the content). And around that, we have a border. BUT, in between the border and the content, is the padding. The padding in the image above is 5px. Just a quick note, you can isolate adding padding to each individual side: top, bottom, left, and right. It is important to remember that the padding is in-between the content and the border. It is easy to get the padding mixed up with the margin.

Now, the margin is the area OUTSIDE of the border. We’ve all seen margins on Microsoft Word or other applications, but the margins are on the outside. Like I said before, try not to get padding and margin mixed up. Padding is inside the border, while margin is outside the border. Again, margins can be isolated to each side. Take a look at the first image for reference!

I hope I have helped clear up any doubts you might have had about borders, padding, or margins. If you are still a little fuzzy about the differences between these properties, I encourage you to try adding padding, margins, and borders to a document and see how they affect the positioning of the elements!
